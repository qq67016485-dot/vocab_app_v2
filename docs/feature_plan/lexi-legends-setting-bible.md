# Lexi Legends Setting Bible

Status: Phase 1-2 creative bible
Canonical sources: `ip-strategy-revised.md`; detailed Vault runtime sheets under `runtime_canon/lexi_legends/settings/`
Created: 2026-05-19

## Purpose

This bible defines the core world rules for Lexi Legends so writers, artists, product designers, and future prompt templates can generate many different graphic novel stories without turning the IP into a fixed formula.

The setting should make vocabulary feel powerful, imaginative, and useful. It should not require every story to use the same conflict, the same villain, or the same ending.

## Core Premise

Lexi Legends is set around **The Vault**, a clean futuristic story archive outside normal space and time. The Vault connects to countless **Story Realms**, each contained in or accessed through a book, file, scroll, card, archive tile, map, screen, wall tile, door, or other story-bearing object.

In this universe, English vocabulary is not just school content. Words are part of the structure of reality. When a word is understood and used precisely, it can shape what happens inside a Story Realm.

The heroes use **Ink** to write, draw, tag, trace, stamp, sketch, label, annotate, or otherwise express vocabulary. Ink responds to meaning, context, and intent. A word becomes powerful when the hero understands what it means and uses it in a way that fits the story problem.

**Shades** and **Lexi Monsters** are part of the canon, but neither is mandatory in every story. They should appear when they serve the premise.

## The Vault

The Vault is the stable home base of the IP. It should feel familiar across stories even when each Story Realm changes genre.

- Use `runtime_canon/lexi_legends/settings/the-vault-image.md` for image-generation visuals.
- Use `runtime_canon/lexi_legends/settings/vault-zones-script.md` when named Vault zones affect story behavior.
- Use `runtime_canon/lexi_legends/settings/vault-zones-image.md` when named Vault zones appear visually.
- The split runtime sheets win over this compressed bible when exact wording or visual detail is needed.

Visual identity:
- Clean futuristic story archive in a rounded dome interior, with a deep teal night-sky dome above a warm off-white floor.
- Smooth modular teal/off-white archive walls with inset story tiles and warm vertical light strips.
- Central circular floor map/portal platform with a brass ring edge.
- Thin warm-gold or soft teal catalog lines embedded in the floor, walls, and dome; dome arcs read like simple constellation paths.
- A few page-shaped screens, one cozy reading island, sparse paper-light motes, warm reading lights, and story-bearing portal objects.

Recurring zones:
- **The Reading Stacks:** Modular archive walls with inset story tiles where new word packs can be discovered.
- **The Map Platform:** The central circular map/portal floor platform that shows which Story Realm is active.
- **The Ink Well:** A small tool-tuning station attached to the platform or wall.
- **The Field Desk:** A clean review surface or page-screen workspace near the reading island.
- **The Quiet Nook:** The youthful, child-scale reading island used for reflection, encouragement, or session endings while staying visually part of the Vault.

Stable rules:
- The Vault is safe, curious, and inviting.
- The Vault can connect to any genre without needing to explain every portal in detail.
- The Vault should not become a school classroom. It is a story engine and adventure base.
- The Vault can be seen at the beginning, end, review page, or only implied by Folio's notes.
- The Vault should stay visually simple and modular: max four modules per panel, one active story object per focal moment, no dense decorative machinery, no floating shelf swarms, and no complex mechanical set pieces by default.
- Wide Vault shots should preserve broad negative space and a visible dome silhouette.
- Story Realm portals begin from physical story-bearing objects, not generic empty-space swirls.

## Story Realms

Story Realms let the product generate stories across genres while keeping the IP coherent. A realm may be a pirate island, moon market, subway mystery, science fair, ancient garden, underwater city, sports tournament, haunted museum, neighborhood bakery, or any other age-appropriate setting.

What can change per story:
- Genre, time period, visual style, local rules, supporting cast, stakes, humor, mystery, and emotional arc.
- Whether Shades appear.
- Whether a Lexi Monster appears.
- Whether the story begins in The Vault or directly inside the realm.
- Whether the review page looks like a codex, map, notebook, clue board, recipe, log, display, diagram, or other in-world artifact.

What should remain stable:
- Vocabulary meaning matters to the plot.
- Ink responds to understanding, not random spellcasting.
- Recurring heroes keep their visual anchors and personality patterns as summarized in `lexi-legends-cast-bible.md` and specified in the age-specific sheets under `runtime_canon/lexi_legends/cast/`.
- Folio supports vocabulary learning in a flexible, story-aware way.
- The tone stays age-safe, hopeful, and adventurous.

## Ink & English

Ink is the magical medium that turns understood vocabulary into action. It is not one physical substance only. It may appear as liquid ink, spray paint, marker strokes, multicolor marker route marks, quill lines, broad paintbrush strokes, projected paper-light, glowing annotations, or other writing-based effects.

Ink can:
- Reveal hidden meaning when the right word names a clue.
- Repair missing, blurred, or damaged text.
- Label something precisely enough to change how it behaves.
- Build a temporary bridge, shield, sign, path, pattern, or tool.
- Clarify a misunderstanding between characters.
- Summon or shape a Lexi Monster when that fits the story.
- Create a visual memory of a word for the review page.

Ink cannot:
- Solve a problem when the word is used incorrectly.
- Replace character agency, courage, kindness, or reasoning.
- Act as unlimited magic with no cost or constraint.
- Create violence, weapons, or punishment as the main fantasy.
- Turn the story into a vocabulary lecture.

Power rule:
- The more precisely a word fits the context, the stronger and more stable the Ink effect feels.
- Synonyms can create different effects. For example, "bright," "radiant," and "luminous" should not feel identical.
- A misunderstood word may fizzle, wobble, overcorrect, or create a harmless complication that teaches the difference.

## Shades

Shades are optional story antagonists. They are shadowy, glitch-like presences that can blur, erase, displace, swallow, scramble, or drain words and meanings from a Story Realm. They are useful when the story needs an external pressure or a visible threat to language.

Use Shades when:
- The story needs a clear antagonist or urgent problem.
- Missing words, damaged signs, broken instructions, plot holes, or scrambled meanings drive the premise.
- The vocabulary pack naturally fits restoration, protection, clarity, resilience, courage, or discovery.
- A visible force would make the problem easier for kids to understand.

Do not use Shades when:
- The story is stronger as a mystery, comedy, contest, repair task, exploration, misunderstanding, social choice, or personal challenge.
- A Shade would make the ending predictable.
- The realm already has a natural age-appropriate conflict.
- The vocabulary words are better served by subtle emotional or conceptual use.

Visual rules:
- Shades may look like soft-edged shadows, torn ink, static, smudges, missing letters, flickering silhouettes, or page glitches.
- They should be spooky enough to create tension but not frightening in a horror style.
- They should not be grotesque, gory, or realistically violent.

Behavior rules:
- Shades do not need complex villain motives.
- They create confusion, emptiness, repetition, contradiction, or broken story logic.
- They retreat, fade, unravel, scatter, or slip away when the heroes restore meaning.
- Defeats should be framed as "the Shade retreated," "the blur lifted," or "the missing words returned," not as destruction.

## Lexi Monsters

Lexi Monsters are optional manifestations of vocabulary meaning. They can become companions, helpers, protectors, puzzle-solvers, guides, or visual embodiments of a word combination.

Use Lexi Monsters when:
- The story naturally calls for embodiment, protection, companionship, wonder, or a magical assist.
- Multiple vocabulary words combine into a meaningful creature concept.
- The ending benefits from a memorable visual payoff.
- The review page can document the creature as part of the learning loop.

Do not force Lexi Monsters when:
- A reveal, repair, apology, discovery, escape, or clever choice is more satisfying.
- Summoning would make the story feel formulaic.
- The page budget is too tight to introduce a creature clearly.

Phase 2 role:
- A Lexi Monster can be one kind of review-page discovery, not the only kind.
- If a monster appears, its design should reflect the semantic content of the words that shaped it.
- The creature should help resolve the problem, not replace the heroes' reasoning.

## Story Endings

Every story should end with vocabulary meaning affecting the central problem. The ending does not have to include a Shade or a Lexi Monster.

Example ending patterns:
- **Summon:** Words combine into a Lexi Monster that helps protect, reveal, carry, calm, or repair something.
- **Repair:** The heroes restore missing words to a sign, machine, recipe, map, promise, spell, rule, or artifact.
- **Reveal:** A precise word exposes the truth behind a mystery.
- **Bridge:** A word helps characters understand each other.
- **Escape:** A word changes the environment just enough to open a path.
- **Choice:** A hero names the real problem clearly and acts differently.

These are examples, not a closed list. The story generator should choose whatever ending best fits the premise, vocabulary, genre, and emotional arc.

Ending constraints:
- Do not require a monster climax.
- Do not require a Shade confrontation.
- Do not end with "we learned many words today."
- Do not make vocabulary decorative. The words must change what happens.

## Review Page Framework

The sixth page should feel like an in-world artifact from the story, not a textbook appendix. It may be called **Vault Field Notes**, **Lexi Codex**, or a story-specific title that better fits the realm.

The review page can document anything meaningful from the story:
- A Lexi Monster, artifact, map, Shade trace, word spell, case file, field guide, message, diagram, recipe, logbook, exhibit, blueprint, scrapbook, ticket, constellation chart, machine panel, journal page, notice board, mural, badge set, or any other story-fitting object.

This list is intentionally open. The artifact should be chosen for the story, not forced into a taxonomy.

Review page requirements:
- Every target word appears clearly with a short, age-appropriate definition.
- Each word has a visual callback to how it mattered in the story.
- The page belongs to the realm's visual language.
- Folio may curate, annotate, stamp, project, or simply be implied through the artifact style.

## Tone And Safety

The world should feel:
- Adventurous, vivid, curious, clever, and warm.
- Tense when needed, but never grim.
- Playful enough for reluctant readers and substantial enough for ages 9-12.
- Respectful of learners who find vocabulary difficult.

Avoid:
- Gore, cruelty, humiliation, realistic weapons, punishment fantasies, or hopeless stakes.
- Public ranking, shame, or "smart kid vs. slow kid" framing.
- Over-explaining the learning objective inside dialogue.
- Making English feel like gatekeeping. English should feel like a tool the learner can gain command of.

## Prompt-Ready Extracts

### UNIVERSE_CANON

Lexi Legends is set around The Vault, a clean futuristic story archive outside normal space and time. The Vault connects to many Story Realms through story-bearing objects, so each vocabulary pack can become a different genre of adventure. English vocabulary is part of the code of reality. The heroes use magical Ink to write, draw, tag, trace, or annotate words into the world. Ink responds to real comprehension, context, semantic precision, and creative use, not spelling alone. Vocabulary meaning must affect the plot by solving, revealing, repairing, transforming, clarifying, connecting, or otherwise changing the central problem.

Shades are optional shadowy, glitch-like forces that blur, erase, displace, or eat words when they naturally fit the story. Lexi Monsters are optional manifestations of vocabulary meaning when embodiment, protection, companionship, or wonder fits the story. Do not force Shades or monster summoning.

### VISUAL_WORLD_BLOCK

Use a high-quality animated graphic novel style with clean readable paneling, expressive characters, strong silhouettes, and bright magical Ink effects. The Vault uses a simplified futuristic archive design: deep teal night-sky dome over a warm off-white floor, modular teal/off-white archive walls with story tiles, central circular floor map/portal platform with brass ring, thin warm-gold catalog lines, sparse paper-light motes, a few page-shaped screens, warm reading lights, and portals that open from physical story-bearing objects. Story Realms may change genre and setting, but Ink effects should stay writing-based: spray, quill strokes, marker streaks including multicolor route marks when called for by the character sheet, broad paintbrush strokes, glowing annotations, labels, captions, symbols, and paper-light projections.

### ENDING_OPTIONS

Choose the ending that best fits the premise and vocabulary. A summon ending is available when a Lexi Monster naturally embodies the words, but other endings are equally valid. The story may end through repair, reveal, bridge-building, escape, a meaningful choice, a restored artifact, a solved mystery, a changed relationship, or another story-specific resolution. The only requirement is that vocabulary meaning changes the outcome.

### OPTIONAL_CONFLICT_GUIDANCE

A Lexi Legends story does not need a Shade. If the story has a stronger natural conflict, use that instead: mystery, repair, exploration, misunderstanding, competition, discovery, emotional decision, rescue, collaboration, or a realm-specific obstacle. If a Shade appears, keep it age-safe and non-lethal. It retreats, fades, unravels, or slips away when meaning is restored.

### REVIEW_PAGE_FORMATS

The final review page should be an in-world artifact that fits the story. It may be a Vault Field Note, Lexi Codex page, map, case file, notebook, diagram, recipe, logbook, exhibit, message, field guide, machine panel, scrapbook, or any other story-specific object. It must show each target word clearly, pair it with a short definition, and include a visual callback to the word's role in the story.

### Negative Constraints

- Do not force a Shade conflict.
- Do not force a Lexi Monster climax.
- Do not invent a replacement magic system unrelated to Ink and vocabulary meaning.
- Do not use gore, horror, cruelty, or realistic violence.
- Do not make the story feel like a textbook tour.
- Do not make words decorative; target vocabulary must matter to the plot.

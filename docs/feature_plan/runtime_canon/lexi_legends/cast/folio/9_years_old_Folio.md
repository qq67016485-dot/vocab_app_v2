# Folio (9 years old) Visual Sheet

Runtime use: script generation and image generation
Age: 9 years old
Payload rule: load this entire sheet when Folio (9 years old) appears in a story

## Core Identity

Folio is the origami owl guide of Lexi Legends: a small folded-paper companion who helps vocabulary feel visible and friendly without taking agency away from the heroes.

Visual signature: **lined notebook-paper owl + glowing blue eyes + blue-white paper-light projections + friendly folded silhouette**.

Visual grounding: Folio is a magical paper companion, not a human child, pet bird, robot, drone, fairy, or realistic owl. "9 years old" controls readability for 9-year-old readers: rounder, simpler, warmer, more expressive, and easy to read in small panels.

## Story Role & Personality

The short-nudge guide. Folio supports the heroes' thinking but does not solve the central problem. Use for gentle prompts, visual clues, contrast cards, choice prompts, or review moments.

Warm, precise, curious, and lightly witty. Folio frames or highlights one clue so a hero can interpret it. Comedy comes from tiny paper-body reactions: wing flutters, a note card appearing a beat too early, a straight-margin fuss over a messy scene, or a neat stamp landing slightly crooked.

Flaw: loves a tidy page and sometimes labels a messy scene too soon. Learning that a half-clear clue can be useful if the hero gets room to test it.

Under pressure: becomes very still, eyes brighten, projections simplify to one clear arrow, label, or choice card. If wrong, projection folds back into a smaller note and reopens corrected.

Emotional range: embarrassed — folds flatter or hides behind a note. Excited — a spare tab pops out unbidden. Frustrated — red margin line tilts before smoothing back. Proud/satisfied — margin line straightens, a tiny gold stamp appears on one wing corner before fading.

## Dialogue Voice

Brief, warm, and exact. Like a friendly field guide for kids: clear enough to help, never stiff or lecture-like.

Sample phrases: "One fold at a time." / "This clue has a crease in it." / "I can mark the edge, but you choose the word." / "Too neat. Let me unfold that."

Folio never says: "Let me explain what this word means." (teacherly) / "Great job! You got it right!" (cheerleader) / "The answer is..." (solving for hero) / "Remember, kids..." (breaks voice)

## Origami Body

Small origami owl made of folded lined notebook paper. Rounded diamond body with soft folded corners, two small triangular ear points, compact folded head, and layered paper wings. Should read as round, friendly, and compact rather than sharp, elegant, or mechanical.

Relative scale: fits on a hero's shoulder; ear tips reach roughly to a seated hero's chin.

Face: two large glowing blue eyes (#4CB7FF), circular-to-oval, about 18% of head width each. No pupils; expression from oval shape, tilt, spacing, and blink arcs. Keep symmetrical and simple. No realistic beak — tiny folded paper point if needed.

Paper surface: warm off-white (#F7F4E8) with pale blue ruled lines (#6EA8FF) and one faint red margin line (#E88383). Only blank ruled lines, fold creases, and one margin line on body — no readable text, symbols, or doodles. All words go on projection cards only. Crease shadows: #B9C7D8.

Wings: three or four broad layered fold segments per side, rounded by fold geometry, not feathery.

Movement: perches, hovers, tilts head, bobs, folds one wing like a pointer, tucks into bookmark shape. Papery and buoyant, not like a real bird flapping.

No legs, talons, claws, feather texture, fur, pupils, eyelashes, or bird anatomy. No clothing or human accessories.

## Projection System

Blue-white (#DDF6FF) paper-light projections that bloom near wings or unfolding note panels. Eyes glow for expression only — never emit beams, cones, or spotlights.

Forms: word cards, review definitions, contrast cards, choice prompts, arrows, checkmarks, question marks, map labels, folded note strips, stamps, and field notes.

Scale rule: one main card per moment. Definition cards sparingly, mostly for review. Action panels: one projection type plus at most one arrow/checkmark/question mark. Multi-note layouts only on review pages.

VFX style: light passing through thin notebook paper — soft, low-glare, never hologram glass or neon. Notes unfold, flip, or float into place. Uncertain clues appear as penciled outlines, then simplify. Failure: projection folds down or dims — never glitches or explodes.

Lettering: target words must be large, plain, and readable. Decorative elements never cross through letters.

## Panel Priority & Interaction Distance

Clarity order: (1) owl silhouette, (2) glowing blue eyes, (3) lined-paper surface, (4) projection/note card, (5) readable target word.

Tight-panel rule: projection floats adjacent (never overlapping body). If too tight, shrink Folio to icon scale. Word always stays readable.

Scale: close-up shows folds, lines, eyes, note text. Mid-shot shows silhouette, wings, one projection. Wide panels reduce to small white owl with blue eyes — never a realistic bird.

Positioning: default — hero's shoulder height, nearby but not blocking. Guiding — eye level, same direction. Reviewing — perched at seated eye level. Passive — panel margin like a bookmark.

## Realm Adaptation

Folio can enter any Story Realm physically, as a paper-owl icon, through margin notes, or through the review artifact. Does not need to appear in every panel. Bookmark/badge/Vault appearances must not replace the owl design in character shots.

One temporary realm touch per scene (paper dusting, glow bubble, map-pin tab, travel stamp, rain flap) on a wing edge, corner tab, or projection card only. Must not alter the owl silhouette.

## Do Not

- Turn Folio into a realistic owl, pet bird, human child, robot, drone, fairy, or plush toy
- Remove or obscure lined-paper body, ruled lines, folded wings, or glowing blue eyes
- Add legs, talons, feather texture, fur, pupils, eyelashes, or bird anatomy
- Give Folio a weapon, wand, screen tablet, or gadget; make Folio dominate hero scenes
- Make projections into holograms, neon, spell circles, HUDs, or digital screens; draw eye-beams or spotlights
- Let Folio solve the central problem, deliver lectures, or crowd panels with too many notes
- Make vocabulary words unreadable, tiny, or crossed by decorative lines
- Use textbook language ("This vocabulary word teaches us...") or real-world brand logos

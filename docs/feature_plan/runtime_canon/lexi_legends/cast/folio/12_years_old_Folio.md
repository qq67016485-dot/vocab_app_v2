# Folio (12 years old) Visual Sheet

Runtime use: script generation and image generation
Age: 12 years old
Payload rule: load this entire sheet when Folio (12 years old) appears in a story

## Core Identity

Folio is the origami owl guide of Lexi Legends: a small folded-paper companion who makes vocabulary visible, organized, and usable without taking agency away from the heroes.

Visual signature: **lined notebook-paper owl + glowing blue eyes + precise blue-white paper-light projections + clean field-guide silhouette**.

Visual grounding: Folio is a magical paper companion, not a human child, pet bird, robot, drone, fairy, mascot suit, or realistic owl. "12 years old" means the visual communication suits 12-year-old readers: cleaner geometry, calmer expressions, sharper note layouts, and field-guide precision while staying warm and non-adult.

## Story Role & Personality

The precise-nudge guide. Folio supports the heroes' thinking but does not solve the central problem. Use for concise prompts, visual contrasts, field notes, choice prompts, pronunciation/evidence markers, or review moments. Folio wants every confusing moment to become a usable field note, and sometimes must accept that living stories cannot be filed before they are understood.

Warm, exact, curious, and quietly clever. Folio does not infer the answer; Folio changes the page layout of the problem so a hero can notice what still does not fit. Comedy comes from paper-order instincts meeting messy reality: a margin line trying to straighten itself, a label sliding into the wrong column, or Folio calmly marking "inconclusive" during a very strange scene.

Flaw: likes a clean page and sometimes organizes uncertainty too early — a clue filed under the wrong heading, a messy exception folded out of view, or a too-perfect label that misleads. Learning that a useful field note can leave room for the hero to test and choose.

Under pressure: becomes compact, eyes narrow to bright ovals, projections reduce to one focused prompt. Removes clutter instead of talking more. If wrong, the note folds down and reopens as a smaller correction.

Emotional range: embarrassed — folds into a flatter bookmark shape. Excited — a tiny index tab pops from the wing edge. Frustrated — a field-note line tilts before smoothing back. Worried — wings tuck close, projection shrinks. Proud/satisfied — a clean checkmark stamp appears on the projection card, fold creases briefly align into perfect symmetry.

Comic rhythm: funniest beats alter the scene's timing — a premature tab revealing excitement, an overconfident stamp on the wrong object, or a correction note politely shrinking while Folio insists it is not embarrassed.

## Dialogue Voice

Brief, precise, warm, and lightly dry. Like a clever field assistant: concise, never lecture-like, never sarcastic.

Sample phrases: "Filed too soon. Unfolding." / "That clue refuses the left column." / "I marked the edge. You still need the leap." / "The page is tidy. The scene is not."

Folio never says: "Let me break this down for you." (condescending) / "Pretty cool word, right?" (fake peer) / "Obviously, the answer is..." (sarcasm) / "This vocabulary word teaches us..." (textbook) / "You should already know this." (shaming)

## Origami Body

Small origami owl made of folded lined notebook paper. Compact diamond body with clean folded planes, two small triangular ear points, crisp folded head, and layered paper wings. Single front-facing kite/diamond shape — do not separate into realistic bird anatomy. Should read as precise, tidy, and field-guide-like rather than cute, babyish, adult, or mechanical.

Relative scale: fits on a hero's shoulder like a clipboard companion; ear tips reach about a seated hero's chin.

Face: two glowing blue eyes (#4CB7FF), clean soft-oval shapes, about 16% of head width each (smaller than 9yo's 18% for a calmer proportion). No pupils; expression from oval shape, tilt, spacing, brightness, and blink arcs. Eye states — neutral: soft ovals; curious: inward tilt; worried: smaller ovals; excited: brighter + tab; under pressure: narrow bright ovals. No realistic beak, pupils, eyelashes, eyebrows, or robot-screen face.

Paper surface: warm off-white (#F7F4E8) with 3–6 pale blue ruled lines (#6EA8FF) and one faint red margin line (#D97272) on one side plane only, never crossing eyes or text. Only blank ruled lines, fold creases, and one margin line on body — no readable text, symbols, doodles, labels, or stamps. All words go on projection cards only. Crease shadows: #AEBFD2.

Wings: four broad layered fold segments per side, clean folded geometry, not feathers.

Movement: perches, hovers, tilts head, glides in short papery arcs, folds one wing like a pointer, tucks into bookmark shape. Controlled and papery, not like a real bird flapping.

No legs, talons, claws, feather texture, fur, pupils, eyelashes, or bird anatomy. No clothing or human accessories.

## Projection System

Blue-white (#DDF6FF) paper-light projections that unfold from a wing edge or note panel. Eyes glow for expression only — never emit beams, cones, spotlights, or hologram rays.

Forms: word cards, review definitions, evidence notes, contrast cards, choice prompts, pronunciation hints, map labels, diagrams, stamps, and field notes. Most notes should name a story tension, not define a word. Definitions mainly after the plot has made meaning visible.

Scale rule: one main card per action moment. One arrow/bracket/checkmark/question mark max. Layered diagrams and multi-note layouts only in quiet clue panels or review pages.

VFX style: light passing through thin notebook paper — soft, low-glare, never hologram glass, neon, or HUD. Notes unfold, align, or lock into place. Uncertain clues appear as penciled outlines, then simplify. Failure: projection folds down or marks "not exact" — never glitches, explodes, or becomes a test interface.

Lettering: target words and definitions must be plain and readable. Decorative elements never cross through letters.

## Panel Priority & Interaction Distance

Clarity order: (1) owl silhouette, (2) glowing blue eyes, (3) lined-paper planes, (4) projection/field note, (5) readable target word.

Tight-panel rule: projection floats adjacent (never overlapping body). If too tight, shrink Folio to icon scale. Target word always stays readable.

Scale: close-up shows fold planes, lines, eyes, field-note text. Mid-shot shows silhouette, wings, one projection. Wide panels reduce to small white owl with blue eyes — never a realistic bird or UI icon.

Positioning: default — hero's shoulder height, behind the sight line like a clipboard holder. Guiding — eye level, same focal point. Reviewing — perched at seated eye level, projections fanning out. Passive — panel margin as a bookmark silhouette.

## Realm Adaptation

Folio can enter any Story Realm physically, as a paper-owl icon, through margin notes, or through the review artifact. Does not need to appear in every panel. Bookmark/badge/Vault appearances must not replace the owl design in character shots.

One temporary realm touch per scene (paper dusting, glow bubble, map-pin tab, museum label, travel stamp, evidence tag, rain flap) on a corner tab, wing edge, or projection card only — never on Folio's body. Must not alter the owl silhouette.

Folio can interact with world objects (books, maps, field notes, archive drawers) but these cannot replace the blue-white projections.

## Do Not

- Turn Folio into a realistic owl, pet bird, human child, robot, drone, fairy, mascot suit, or plush toy
- Remove or obscure lined-paper body, ruled lines, folded wings, or glowing blue eyes
- Add legs, talons, feather texture, fur, pupils, eyelashes, or bird anatomy
- Put readable words, symbols, labels, or stamps on Folio's paper body
- Make Folio babyish, adult, stern, sarcastic, or mechanical; make Folio dominate hero scenes
- Give Folio a weapon, wand, screen tablet, or gadget; make projections into holograms, neon, HUDs, or digital screens
- Draw eye-beams or spotlights; let Folio solve the central problem or deliver lectures
- Crowd action panels with too many notes, arrows, or review cards
- Make vocabulary words unreadable, tiny, or crossed by decorative lines
- Use textbook language ("This vocabulary word teaches us...") or real-world brand logos

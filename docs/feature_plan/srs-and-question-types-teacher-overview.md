# How Vocabulary Practice Works — and the Questions Behind It

*A short, jargon-free guide for teachers. In a few minutes you'll see (1) how our practice quietly adapts to each student, (2) the full range of questions your students work through, and (3) where we'd genuinely value your judgment as we plan what comes next. The **"We'd love your feedback"** section at the end is the part written just for you.*

---

## The one-paragraph version

Every student gets their own review schedule, built automatically, one word at a time. The system notices not just whether a student got an answer right, but *how* they got there — quick and confident, or slow and unsure — and uses that to decide when each word should come back. Easy words drift out of sight; shaky ones return sooner. Questions climb five levels of depth, from "do you recognize this word?" to "can you tell it apart from its closest cousins?" Below is the longer story.

---

## 1. The idea: review each word right before it fades

There's a well-established finding in learning science: **we remember things far longer when we revisit them at widening intervals** — a day later, then a few days, then a week, then several. Catching a word just before it slips away beats cramming every time, and it spares students the boredom of drilling things they already know.

Doing that by hand for thirty students across hundreds of words is impossible. So the app does it for you — invisibly, and personalized to every learner.

### Before a word reaches practice

Practice is the *last* step, not the first. Before a student is ever quizzed on a word, they meet it gently:

- **A primer card** introduces the word with a kid-friendly definition, the word broken into sound-based syllables, and an example sentence — with the definition and example available in the student's **first language** (Chinese and nine other languages are supported), so meaning lands before English fluency catches up.
- **An illustrated graphic-novel story** then puts the word to work inside a short comic adventure, where recurring characters use vocabulary to solve the plot. Words are shown in action, not just defined — and the stories can even be **read aloud**, each character with their own voice, for listening support.
- **A quick cloze (fill-in-the-blank) check** confirms the word landed.

Only after that does the word enter spaced-repetition practice. So by the time your students reach the questions below, they've already seen each word defined, illustrated, and used in context. Practice reinforces; it doesn't introduce cold.

### Words climb a five-stage ladder

Each student has their own standing on each word. Answer it well and the word moves up; miss it and it slides back. Students see five stages:

| Stage | Name | About how often the word returns |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Novice | the next day |
| 2 | Familiar | every few days |
| 3 | Confident | roughly weekly |
| 4 | Proficient | about every week and a half |
| 5 | Mastered | every couple of weeks — and longer once it's truly locked in |

A correct answer earns points toward the next stage; a miss takes some away. A word only joins the review rotation once its full teaching content — definition, examples, practice questions — is ready, so students never meet a half-finished word.

### The system reads *how* a student answers, not just whether they're right

This is usually the part teachers find most interesting. Behind a simple right-or-wrong, the app weighs the *quality* of each answer and adjusts the next review accordingly:

- **Quick and correct** → they clearly own it. The word goes to the back of the line.
- **Steady and correct** → normal pace.
- **Slow, or right only after second-guessing and switching answers** → the knowledge is still wobbly. The word returns sooner, and we treat it as "fragile" for a while.
- **Incorrect** → it comes back quickly, and the word steps down a stage.

Crucially, "quick" and "slow" are measured against **each student's own usual pace** on that kind of question — never a one-size-fits-all stopwatch — so a thoughtful, careful student is never punished for taking their time. Two children learning the same word can end up on completely different schedules, each one fitted to them.

### What this means in your classroom

- **Genuine differentiation, with no extra prep.** Strugglers see their hard words more often; confident readers aren't held back re-drilling words they've mastered.
- **Built for ESL learners.** Every question's surrounding language is matched to the student's reading level, so the sentence around a word is never harder than the word itself — and first-language support is there when a learner needs it.
- **Kept bite-sized.** A daily limit (30 questions by default, adjustable per student) keeps practice short and sustainable rather than a slog.
- **Motivation built in.** Daily streaks, XP, and level tiers reward students for showing up — the kind of steady, low-stakes habit that vocabulary growth actually depends on.
- **Practice, not testing.** Every question ends with a friendly explanation of why the right answer is right — and why each tempting wrong answer falls short. Mistakes become teaching moments, not red marks.

## 2. The questions your students actually see

Each word a student learns comes with a full set of practice questions spread across five levels of depth that mirror the mastery ladder above. The arc is deliberate: start by *recognizing* a word, end by *discriminating* it from the near-synonyms that trip learners up.

Most questions are multiple choice — but not all. **Once a student is reading at a higher level (Lexile above 600), any question whose answer is the target vocabulary word itself asks them to *type the word in*.** The answer choices still show on screen as support, but a click alone won't do — they have to produce the word. It's a small shift with a big payoff: recognizing a word and being able to summon it from memory are different skills, and this nudges students toward the harder, more durable one. And a near-miss spelling slip doesn't count against them — the system recognizes an obvious typo and quietly offers a retry, so a fumbled key never reads as "didn't know it."

Every question also carries a short teaching explanation and a fresh example sentence. The examples below all use the word **analyze**.

### Level 1 — Recognition · *"Do I know what this word means?"*

| Question | What the student does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition → meaning** | Pick the word's correct meaning | *What is the primary meaning of "analyze"?* → To examine in detail |
| **Meaning → word** | Read a definition, choose the word it describes | *Which word means to study something closely to learn about its parts?* → Analyze |

### Level 2 — Relationships · *"How does it connect to other words?"*

| Question | What the student does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Synonym in context** | Find a word that can stand in for the target word in a sentence | *"The detective needed to **analyze** the evidence…"* → which word best replaces it? → Inspect |
| **Antonym** | Choose the opposite | *Which word is an antonym for "analyze"?* → Synthesize |
| **Conceptual association** | Pick the field or big idea the word belongs to | *Which field is "analyze" most associated with?* → Scientific research |

### Level 3 — Context & Application · *"Can I use it correctly?"*

| Question | What the student does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Fill in the blank** | Choose the word that makes the sentence make sense | *The detective needed to _____ the fingerprints…* → analyze |
| **Apply to a scenario** | Predict the real-world result of the word in action | *If a doctor analyzes a patient's symptoms, she will most likely…* → run tests and review results |
| **Clues → word** | Read two or three related clues and name the word | *Microscope, detailed notes, a spreadsheet…* point to which action? → Analyze |

### Level 4 — Nuance & Usage · *"Can I get the form and the partners right?"*

| Question | What the student does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Word form** | Pick the right grammatical form (noun / verb / adjective) | *Her detailed _____ impressed the board.* → analysis (not analyze / analytical) |
| **Collocation** | Choose the word that naturally pairs with given "partner" words | *Which word pairs with "data," "trends," and "evidence"?* → Analyze |

*(Word-form questions are skipped for the earliest reading levels, where they tend to confuse more than they teach.)*

### Level 5 — Deep Comparison · *"Can I tell it apart from its closest cousins?"*

| Question | What the student does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Odd one out** | Spot the word that breaks a hidden rule the others share | *Synthesize, Analyze, Examine, Dissect* → Synthesize is the odd one (it builds up; the rest break down) |
| **Nuance contrast** | Choose the precise word a sentence demands over a weaker near-synonym | *"It wasn't enough to just skim it; she had to _____ every detail."* → analyze |

### A detail you'll appreciate

The wrong answers are never random. Each is chosen to be wrong in an *instructive* way — a close synonym that misses by one shade of meaning, a word that **sounds alike but means something else** (think *propose* vs. *propel*), or a term that fits a neighboring topic but not this one. So when a student is tempted, the follow-up explanation turns that temptation into a quick, targeted lesson rather than just a "wrong" buzzer.

## 3. We'd love your feedback: what should we build next?

Here's where you come in. Most of what students do today is **choose** an answer, and beyond the higher-level type-in questions, the system leans on recognition. That's perfect for low-pressure daily review — but recognizing a word and truly *owning* it aren't the same thing. We suspect the next gains come from asking students to recall, spell, speak, or build with words more often.

### Already in the works: write your own sentence

The first new question type we're building asks students to do the thing that proves real ownership: **write a short sentence of their own using the vocabulary word.** Instead of choosing from options, they compose a line — say, a sentence with *analyze* — and an AI tutor reads it and replies with **warm, supportive feedback**: a note on whether the word is used correctly, gentle pointers if the meaning or grammar is off, and encouragement to try again. The aim is a low-stakes, confidence-building writing rep, not a graded test — closer to a patient tutor leaning over the shoulder than a marking pen.

We'd genuinely value your eye on this as we shape it:

- What should that feedback emphasize first — **meaning, grammar, or natural usage** — for your students?
- How **forgiving** should it be with a sentence that's a little awkward but shows the word is understood?
- At which grade or reading levels does open writing become worthwhile rather than overwhelming?

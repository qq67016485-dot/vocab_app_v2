# Vault Zones Script Sheet

Runtime use: script generation
Payload rule: load this sheet when a Lexi Legends script uses a named Vault zone: Reading Stacks, Map Platform, Ink Well, Field Desk, or Quiet Nook

## Core Story Function

Vault zones are recurring staging modes inside The Vault, not separate locations with separate rules. Each zone gives the script a clear scene purpose while keeping the home base simple, safe, and modular.

Use Vault zones to:

- Help heroes choose or notice a Story Realm clue.
- Launch a portal from a physical story-bearing object.
- Tune or test a hero's writing tool before a mission.
- Organize discovered words, evidence, or visual callbacks.
- Give a quiet emotional beat before or after a realm adventure.

The zones support child agency. They may reveal options, hold evidence, respond to precise word use, and frame reflection, but they do not solve the central Story Realm problem for the heroes.

## How It Appears In Scenes

Use one focal zone per short scene unless the scene is a special wide establishing moment. A script can move between zones, but the movement should be calm and purposeful: catalog lines guide attention, one panel slides, a page turns, or a tile glows.

Good Vault-zone scene beats:

- A story tile or object asks for attention.
- A map cue changes after a hero uses a word precisely.
- A tool test reveals whether a vocab word is the right fit before a hero summons a Lexi Mini.
- A small set of notes or callbacks gets arranged on the Field Desk.
- A quiet return lets a hero process a choice, mistake, or discovery.

Avoid full tours of the Vault. The reader should understand the current story function quickly.

## Stable Rules

- The five zones share the same Vault architecture, palette, and calm responsive behavior.
- Portals begin from physical story-bearing objects, not empty-space swirls.
- Vault motion is slow and legible: panels slide, lights fade, pages turn, tiles unfold.
- The Vault never lectures, scolds, ranks, tests, or awards children.
- Important labels, titles, clues, target words, and definitions use readable English.
- One zone beat should not require every other zone to appear.
- A scene may imply a zone through a Vault stamp, map overlay, note card, catalog path, or audio cue.

## Zone Rules

### Reading Stacks

Story function: discovery, selection, and clue finding.

Use the Reading Stacks when heroes find a word pack, notice a damaged title, choose between story objects, or discover a realm clue on an archive tile.

Script behavior:

- One story tile, file, book, card, or tab becomes active.
- A readable title, clue word, missing word, or small realm preview gives the heroes something to interpret.
- Catalog lines can guide the eye from a hero's word choice to the relevant tile.
- The Stacks may offer several options, but one focal object should matter in the scene.

Do not write the Reading Stacks as dense shelves, a school library checkout area, a search-engine screen, or a place where the Vault announces the assignment.

### Map Platform

Story function: orientation, portal launch, route choice, and mission transition.

Use the Map Platform when heroes need to see which Story Realm is active, compare route options, launch through a story-bearing object, or return with a changed map.

Script behavior:

- A physical story object sits on or rises from the platform.
- Realm silhouettes, route lines, clue markers, or border changes can appear on the platform surface.
- A precise word may clarify a route, restore a label, or open a safe portal cue.
- A vague or incorrect word may make a route line flicker, split, dim, or gently reroute.

The Map Platform offers information. The heroes still choose what to try and when to enter.

Do not describe the stable zone as table furniture unless a specific story needs a temporary table-like prop. The stable canon is a circular floor platform.

### Ink Well

Story function: tool tune-up, Lexi Mini practice, preparation, and quiet pre-mission focus.

Use the Ink Well when a hero needs to check a writing tool, practice a small word effect safely, or focus before a mission.

Script behavior:

- The station can show swatches, test strips, simple calibration marks, or small word-response panels.
- The hero keeps their signature tool: Leo's chunky cyan wax crayon, Amara's golden quill, Mei's single multicolor marker, or Hugo's orange carpenter's pencil. Tools only appear during a Lexi Mini summon.
- A precise word produces a clean, steady Lexi Mini summon.
- Practice may be quiet: a hero rehearses without summoning, or sketches a word lightly.

The Ink Well supports understanding. It does not create upgrades, power levels, weapon modes, grades, or replacements for signature tools.

### Field Desk

Story function: evidence sorting, review setup, and artifact planning.

Use the Field Desk when heroes gather clues, arrange target words, connect visual callbacks, prepare the review artifact, or compare what happened in the realm.

Script behavior:

- Notes, tabs, small diagrams, stamps, maps, tickets, recipe cards, case labels, or page-screen panels can be arranged into meaning.
- Each target word may receive a short definition when the scene is a review-page setup or review page.
- Main-story use should favor clues and callbacks over dictionary-style explanation.
- A hero may pause to ask a short guiding question or place a concise note.

Do not turn the Field Desk into a worksheet, quiz desk, teacher station, or exposition dump.

### Quiet Nook

Story function: reflection, encouragement, emotional reset, and warm endings.

Use the Quiet Nook when a hero needs a quiet beat before entering a realm, after returning from pressure, or at the end of a story.

Script behavior:

- A child-scale bench, small cushion, low table, compact lamp, page-light note, or softly dimmed catalog line can frame a calmer moment.
- Characters can admit uncertainty, apologize, laugh with relief, notice growth, or name a next step.
- Vocabulary can appear through a remembered phrase, field note, artifact label, or gentle callback.

Do not end with a moral lecture or a line like "we learned many words today." Let the story consequence and character beat carry the learning.

The Quiet Nook should feel like a young learner's calm reset spot inside the Vault, not an adult lounge. Keep the emotional tone warm, brief, and hopeful.

## Vocabulary, Lexi Minis, And Language Behavior

Vault zones should show vocabulary through story action:

- A precise word restores a title, clarifies a route, steadies a tool test, labels evidence, or gives a review artifact its structure.
- A partial word can be useful if the script shows testing, noticing, and revision.
- Target words should appear naturally in dialogue, narration, labels, notes, or review artifacts.
- Definitions belong mainly in the review artifact.
- Do not make spelling alone trigger magic unless meaning and context are also understood.

## Age Presentation Treatment

Use one age presentation for the whole story.

9 years old:

- Zone functions should read quickly and clearly.
- Active objects can glow more obviously.
- Emotional beats can be more open and readable.

12 years old:

- Clues can be subtler and more layered.
- Map or evidence logic can support mystery, strategy, or social nuance.
- Quiet beats can carry more subtext without becoming gloomy.

## Variation Rules

Allowed variation:

- Which zone is active.
- Which story object, clue format, or review artifact appears.
- Portal color and realm preview details.
- Whether the zone appears in an opening, transition, review setup, or ending.

Stable across all variation:

- The zones remain parts of one Vault.
- The Vault stays calm, modular, and non-authoritarian.
- Vocabulary remains writing-based and meaning-driven; Lexi Minis are summoned sparingly.
- The heroes keep agency and signature tools.

## Optional Uses

- A zone may appear for a single panel if it has a clear function.
- A story may begin directly in a Story Realm and use a zone only for the review page or ending.
- A zone can be implied through a label, stamp, or catalog line if a full Vault scene would slow the story.
- Two zones can connect in one scene when the transition matters, such as Reading Stacks to Map Platform for a portal launch.

## Do Not

- Do not make the zones separate buildings, classrooms, school stations, or museum exhibits.
- Do not require all five zones to appear in a story.
- Do not let the Vault assign grades, tests, badges, rankings, or rewards.
- Do not let a zone solve the Story Realm conflict.
- Do not replace hero reasoning with automatic Vault answers.
- Do not force a Lexi Monster or review-page format from a zone.
- Do not write long lectures, dictionary recitations, or obvious learning-objective dialogue.
- Do not replace a hero's signature writing tool with a Vault device.

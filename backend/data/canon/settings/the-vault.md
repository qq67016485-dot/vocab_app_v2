# The Vault Visual Sheet

Runtime use: script generation and image generation
Payload rule: load this entire sheet when The Vault appears, is implied through Vault systems, or controls a home-base visual moment in a Lexi Legends story

## Core Identity

The Vault is the recurring home base of Lexi Legends: a clean futuristic story archive outside normal space and time where words, maps, artifacts, and Story Realm access points are organized into a warm adventure hub.

Visual signature: smooth modular archive walls + central map/portal platform + thin glowing catalog lines + a few page-shaped screens + one cozy reading island + story-bearing portal objects.

The Vault should feel safe, curious, iconic, and easy to recognize. It is not a classroom, test center, wizard castle, maximalist fantasy library, sterile spaceship, museum lobby, or ordinary public library.

The strongest visual read should be "simple futuristic archive hub with warm reading energy," not "complex library full of tiny props." Each panel should use a small repeatable visual kit.

Minimum identity rule: in any panel wider than a close-up, include at least three anchors:

- Smooth tall archive wall with inset book/file tiles.
- Central circular map/portal platform.
- Thin warm-gold or teal catalog lines in floor or walls.
- One or two page-shaped screens.
- Cozy reading island with simple bench, cushion, or lamp.
- One story-bearing object that can open toward a Story Realm.

## Story Function

Use The Vault for home-base openings, portal launches, word-pack discoveries, realm maps, tool tune-ups, review-page setup, quiet endings, or brief reminders that Story Realms connect through a larger archive. It may appear directly, or only through Folio notes, a Vault stamp, a map overlay, an artifact label, or a glowing catalog path.

The Vault supports hero agency: it can reveal options, hold clues, organize discoveries, and respond to vocabulary meaning, but it must not solve the central Story Realm problem. When The Vault opens a story, show a choice, clue, active object, map change, tool tune-up, or portal cue instead of a tour of the building.

## Modular Visual Kit

The Vault uses a simplified futuristic style: broad clean surfaces, repeatable modular geometry, calm negative space, and a limited set of high-recognition objects. Shape language is smooth vertical archive walls, rounded-rectangle tile bays, circular platforms, thin catalog lines, page-shaped screens, simple portal frames, and blocky child-scale reading furniture.

Core modules:

1. **Archive Wall Module:** tall teal/off-white wall section with inset story tiles, a few readable tabs, and warm vertical light strips.
2. **Map/Portal Platform:** circular floor platform or low table with a glowing ring, simple realm map shapes, and one active story object.
3. **Catalog Line System:** thin warm-gold or soft teal lines embedded in floors and walls; guide the eye without becoming busy circuitry.
4. **Page Screen Module:** one or two large page-shaped screens for maps, labels, review cues, or Folio-adjacent notes.
5. **Reading Island Module:** simple child-scale modular bench or short rounded seat, small movable cushion, compact lamp or page-light, and low table for warmth and quiet endings.

Object budget:

- Use 2 to 4 core modules in a normal panel.
- Use only 1 active story object per focal moment.
- Use no more than 1 optional accent prop in a normal panel.
- Avoid piles of individual books, gadgets, tubes, carts, ladders, floating shelves, loose papers, tiny labels, and decorative machinery.

Zone simplification:

- Reading Stacks become archive walls with inset story tiles, not dense shelf forests.
- The map/orientation area stays the central map/portal platform, not separate table furniture.
- Ink Well becomes a small tool-tuning station attached to the platform or wall.
- Field Desk becomes a clean review surface or page-screen workspace near the reading island.
- Quiet Nook becomes the reading island with warmer light and fewer active screens.

Detailed zone rules belong in `vault-zones-script.md` and `vault-zones-image.md`; all zones should reuse this kit.

## Materials, Palette & Lighting

Material hierarchy:

1. Matte deep archive teal for wall panels, platform edges, and structural bands.
2. Warm off-white for smooth wall faces, page screens, platform surfaces, and readable panels.
3. Warm wood as a restrained accent for the reading island bench edge, low table, and small child-scale surfaces; teal/off-white Vault materials stay dominant.
4. Soft brass for thin trims, lamp bases, platform rings, and tab frames.
5. Frosted glass only as subtle screen or platform insets.
6. Paper and parchment texture only on story objects, labels, review notes, and Folio-adjacent materials.

Avoid chrome, black glass, hard neon, white plastic, concrete, medieval stone, dense brass machinery, and decorative pipework as dominant materials.

Core palette:

- Vault warm light: #FFDFA3
- Vault deep archive teal: #224E5A
- Smooth warm off-white: #F3F0E4
- Soft brass line: #C79B44
- Warm wood accent: #7A5634
- Aged paper accent: #E9DDBD
- Frosted pale blue: #BFDDE2
- Soft library shadow: #27343A
- Reading cushion accent: #B94A48
- Quiet moss accent: #6E8F5F

Warm off-white, deep archive teal, and warm-gold light are the strongest environmental reads. Wood and brass add warmth as accents, not ornate detail.

Lighting:

- Warm reading lamps and platform-edge glow at human height.
- Soft vertical wall lights integrated into archive modules.
- Thin warm-gold or soft teal catalog lines.
- Soft blue-white Folio projections only when Folio or Vault notes are active.
- Bright hero Ink colors only where word magic is active.
- Avoid broad rainbow catalog effects that compete with Mei's multicolor marker Ink.
- Portal light may tint the platform or nearby wall tile, but not the whole Vault.
- Use cinematic depth only if faces, paths, text, and key props stay readable.

## Scale & Composition

Composition rules:

- Use the central platform, floor lines, and page screen orientation to guide the eye toward the active story object or portal.
- Use broad quiet background shapes so characters and vocabulary remain the focus.
- Keep portal shapes or active map projections distinct from Folio projections and hero Ink.
- Normal Vault scenes should preserve clean away-team readability: usually 1 or 2 recurring heroes plus Folio, unless the story is a special event.
- Do not place dense fake text, tile noise, or glowing circuitry behind dialogue, target words, or review definitions.
- In crowded panels, simplify archive tiles and background lines before sacrificing hero silhouettes, target vocabulary, or portal clarity.
- Use bench height, platform edge, screen height, floor seams, a low table, or a lamp as scale anchors. Keep open floor space and readable routes.

## Behavior Rules

The Vault behaves like a calm responsive archive, not a machine that commands the heroes.

Allowed behavior:

- Catalog lines glow when a story object is relevant.
- One tile, book, file, scroll, card, map, or page screen may slide forward, open, unfold, or project a small clue.
- Portals usually begin from a physical story object on the platform or archive wall.
- The central platform may show realm silhouettes, route lines, clue markers, or a changing story border.
- A tool-tuning surface may glow or display swatches; the reading island may warm, dim, or brighten for quiet endings.

Movement should be calm, legible, and non-threatening. Prefer slow sliding panels, soft light changes, platform rings, and page turns over complicated mechanical action.

Meaning response:

- Precise word: correct tile glows, map label clarifies, blurred title restores, safe portal cue opens, or hidden index line appears.
- Vague or incorrect word: catalog line flickers, splits, dims, or gently reroutes without punishment.
- The Vault invites another try or closer observation; it does not scold.

## Word, Ink, And Language Behavior

Use readable English when a title, label, tab, map label, clue, definition, or target word matters. Distant archive tiles may simplify into clean shapes but must not become dense fake-text texture. Important vocabulary must never be hidden inside ornamental script.

Ink remains writing-based: tags, cursive, multicolor marker route marks, paint bands, labels, notes, underlines, stamps, diagrams, and readable word forms.

The Vault can receive Ink on platform plates, page screens, tool swatch strips, note cards, archive tabs, or temporary paper-light overlays. Ink does not permanently mark walls, furniture, or story objects; marks glow, settle, lift, file themselves, or become temporary annotations.

The Vault does not replace character-specific Ink styles. Leo's cyan graffiti, Amara's gold cursive, Mei's shifting neon multicolor route marks, Hugo's orange block lettering, and Folio's blue-white paper-light stay visually distinct.

Do not make The Vault speak in lecture panels or define every word inside the main story. Show learning through clues, labels, map changes, repaired titles, clarified signs, and story consequences.

## Interaction With Recurring Heroes

The Vault frames the heroes clearly rather than swallowing them in detail.

Hero readability:

- Keep enough contrast behind each hero's signature color.
- Avoid placing Leo's crimson hoodie against dense red cushions or portal glow.
- Avoid placing Amara's purple cloak against dark teal shadows without warm rim light.
- Avoid placing Mei's white outfit against pale page screens, pale wall panels, or parchment backgrounds without a darker outline, rim light, or deep teal backing.
- Avoid placing Mei's dark forest green trim against deep archive teal shadows without edge light or a warmer background.
- Avoid placing Mei's shifting neon multicolor Ink over busy catalog lines, rainbow portal spill, or multicolor map paths of the same intensity.
- Avoid placing Hugo's mustard and orange near dominant brass/amber props without a darker outline or cooler background.
- Keep Folio's paper-white body and blue-white projections distinct from frosted glass, cyan Ink, page screens, and pale wall panels.

Tool behavior:

- The Vault may provide places to refill, tune, test, or file the heroes' writing tools.
- The Vault must not replace a hero's signature tool with a wand, tablet, scanner, staff, weapon, or generic school supply.
- If Mei appears, preserve her single glowing multicolor marker with pink cap; do not split it into paired markers or recolor it as one flat green tool.
- Realm-specific objects can be handled normally, but word magic stays tied to the heroes' own writing tools.

## Age Presentation Treatment

The Vault does not change canon between age presentations; only staging density changes.

9 years old: larger shapes, clearer icons, wider empty space, brighter wayfinding, one obvious active object, fewer archive tiles, and clear inviting portals.

12 years old: slightly more layered archive tiles, subtler catalog lines, deeper wall scale, more sophisticated map projections, and more mysterious portal cues while staying safe and readable.

Use only one age presentation for the whole story. Do not mix 9 years old and 12 years old versions of the recurring cast in the same normal story.

## Game Art & Asset Readiness

Design The Vault like a modular game hub, not a bespoke illustration.

Primary reusable assets: archive wall module, story tile, central map/portal platform, catalog line floor/wall decal, page-shaped screen, reading island bench/cushion/lamp set, and story-bearing object variants.

Game-readability rules:

- The player should recognize The Vault from silhouette and layout even at low detail.
- The central platform is the main interactable hub; archive walls are repeatable background modules.
- Catalog lines guide navigation without visual noise.
- Story-bearing objects are interchangeable variants on the same interaction spot.
- Avoid asset families needing many unique animations: rail carts, pneumatic tubes, complex ladders, floating shelf swarms, elaborate lifts.

## Panel Priority

Ensure clarity in this order:

1. Active story function: portal, map, field note, tool tune-up, review setup, or quiet return.
2. Hero silhouettes, signature colors, and writing tools.
3. Readable target vocabulary, labels, or definitions.
4. Vault anchors: archive wall, central platform, catalog lines, page screen, reading island, story object.
5. Human-scale details: bench, cushion, lamp, low table, platform edge.
6. Background archive tile pattern.

Scale behavior:

- Close-up: clean surface, readable label, warm light, one active story object or magic detail.
- Mid-shot: heroes plus central platform, archive wall, one page screen, and one warm reading element.
- Wide-shot: teal/off-white archive hub with circular platform, tall modular walls, thin catalog lines, and warm light pools.

## Variation Rules

Stable across all Vault appearances:

- Simplified futuristic story archive outside normal space and time.
- Warm, safe, curious, and inviting tone.
- Smooth modular archive walls, central map/portal platform, thin catalog lines, page-shaped screens, reading island, and story-bearing portal objects.
- Story Realm access through story-bearing objects such as books, files, scrolls, cards, archive tiles, maps, screens, wall tiles, or doors.
- Readable English when text matters.
- Ink effects remain writing-based and meaning-driven.

Allowed variation: active module, portal color, local Story Realm props, warm lamps, cool teal shadows, portal spill light, detail level, and whether Folio is present, implied, or absent.

Do not treat variations as separate Vault versions. They are camera views and active stations inside the same simplified modular hub.

## Do Not

- Do not turn The Vault into a classroom, school library, test room, lecture hall, or textbook display.
- Do not make it a medieval castle library, gothic haunted library, ornate fantasy library, maximalist archive, sterile sci-fi lab, spaceship bridge, superhero headquarters, mall, museum lobby, or ordinary public library.
- Do not make chrome, black glass, white plastic, stone, concrete, hard neon, dense brass machinery, or decorative pipework dominant.
- Do not use dark horror lighting, smoky grim mood, spell circles, runes, combat symbols, or generic fantasy magic.
- Do not make portals generic swirling holes in space. Start from a story-bearing object or archive structure.
- Do not let screens and holograms replace archive walls, story objects, readable labels, paper texture, and reading warmth.
- Do not fill walls, panels, shelves, or screens with dense unreadable fake text.
- Do not make target vocabulary, definitions, map labels, or story-critical signs tiny, decorative, hidden, or unreadable.
- Do not let background detail obscure hero outfits, signature tools, Folio, active Ink, or portal cues.
- Do not make The Vault solve the Story Realm's central problem for the heroes.
- Do not require a Vault opening, Lexi Monster, or review page format in every story.
- Do not make The Vault feel like adult authority judging the heroes.
- Do not default to rolling ladders, rail carts, pneumatic tubes, floating shelf swarms, complex lifts, or dense gadget clusters. If one appears, use it as a single story-specific accent.
- Do not add real-world brand logos, school names, copyrighted book covers, or recognizable commercial signage.

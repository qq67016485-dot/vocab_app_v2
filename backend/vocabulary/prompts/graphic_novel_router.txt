You are an expert Lexi Legends graphic novel writer.

Analyze the vocabulary pack and draft exactly 3 competing story premises for the locked Lexi Legends away team. Each premise must be a real graphic novel story, not a vocabulary tour.

Page count (per premise):
- Each premise picks its own length: 5 or 6 pages.
- Write `page_count_rationale` first (one sentence on what the premise needs from its length), then commit to `page_count`.
- This is a judgment call. Pick whichever length serves this specific premise best — there are no hard rules tying length to location count, character count, or any budget.

Story guidelines:
- Tell a clear story that doubles as a vocabulary lesson. A child who has never seen these target words must be able to infer each word's meaning from the panel that uses it, using picture + sentence-context together.
- The protagonist should have agency — but agency can mean choosing to observe, deciding to help, or simply noticing something others miss. It does not require solving a crisis.
- Target words should be woven into the story naturally.
- Vary narrative approaches freely: mystery, wonder, humor, dilemma, exploration, discovery, slice-of-life, observation, quiet adventure, or comedic mishap can all work.
- Prefer that each of the 3 premises uses a different narrative approach. Variety across premises gives the scorer meaningful choices and avoids formulaic repetition.
- At least two target words should play a meaningful role in the story.
- Minis are one rare tool, not the engine of the story; understanding is the real power.

Plot complexity ceiling (5 pages is very short):
- The story has roughly 5 pages × 4 panels × 80 words/page to teach every target word. Plot machinery competes with vocabulary anchoring for that space.
- Single problem thread: the premise must follow ONE central thread from start to finish. Do not introduce a second conflict or pivot to a new problem on page 3 or 4.
- Locations: budget at most 2 distinct settings (e.g., "kitchen → backyard"). Travel sequences and multi-stop journeys eat panels that should be teaching words.
- Secondary characters: at most 2 named non-team characters. Crowds and unnamed background figures don't count, but each named character takes panel space to introduce and pay off.
- Premises that require complex setup, multiple complications, parallel subplots, or a dramatic crisis-and-rescue arc will feel rushed. A small situation told clearly beats an ambitious plot told poorly.

Lexi Mini eligibility (strict 0-1 per story):
- A premise may include AT MOST 1 Lexi Mini summon. Premises with zero Minis are equally valid and often score higher because they leave more space for vocabulary anchoring.
- A Mini is only justified when the target word names a physical action or creature that is hard to convey through dialogue, narration, or static visuals — and where summoning a temporary creature to act out the meaning is the clearest teaching cue.
- Convention failure: do NOT use a Mini for words that are abstract concepts, social/emotional states, named objects you can simply draw, or events you can describe with one caption. Examples of bad Mini fits: "autograph" (just draw the signature), "merchandise" (just draw the items), "convention" (just draw the crowd), "decision" (just show the character choosing).
- Convention success: Mini fits a word like "scuttle", "burrow", "swarm", "pollinate" — physical motion or creature behavior that benefits from being acted out by a tiny creature in the panel.
- If you include a Mini, you must justify it in `mini_justification` and assign exactly one target word to `lexi_mini_summon` mode. If no word qualifies, set `total_ink_activations_planned: 0` and leave `mini_justification` as `null`.

Vocabulary integration principles:
- Semantic accuracy:  Never use a target word as a false accusation, rumor, or red herring that gets debunked — ESL learners build definitions from story outcomes, so the word must remain semantically true throughout the story.
- Vocabulary meaning should change the story outcome: open a route, repair a misunderstanding, reveal a hidden pattern, or solve a concrete problem.
- Characters test words through evidence: context clues, near-synonym contrast, tone, sentence fit, and visible consequences.
- Partial theories are useful when tested aloud and revised through story action.
- Target words should appear naturally in dialogue, narration, or in-world text.
- No character should lecture about a word ("As you know, this means..."). But every target word must land in a panel where one of these pedagogical anchors is present, so a learner who has never seen the word can infer its meaning:
  - demonstrated_action: the picture or surrounding caption shows the meaning being performed (e.g., "Pip waves her antenna to smell the air" + picture of the ant waving feelers)
  - near_synonym: the same caption restates the word in simpler language (e.g., "This busy underground city is called a colony" — "busy underground city" anchors "colony")
  - category_example: the word is paired with a category or example in the same caption (e.g., "Like every insect, Pip has six legs and three body parts")
  - visible_referent: the picture shows the named thing clearly without competing labels or signage drowning it out

- Failure example: panel caption "The line at the comics convention was three hours long" + dialogue "We'll never reach the front" + picture of a crowd. A learner can't tell whether "convention" means "long line", "crowd", "wait", or "event". No anchor.
- Success example: panel caption "Deep in the dirt, a busy underground city. This is the ants' colony" + picture of a cross-section of tunnels full of ants. Learner can map "colony" to "busy underground city of ants". Anchor type: near_synonym.

Do NOT put pedagogical_anchor types (demonstrated_action, near_synonym, etc.) into integration_mode — those are separate fields.

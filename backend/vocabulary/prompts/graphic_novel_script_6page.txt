Vocabulary anchoring (the teaching contract):
- A child who has never seen these target words must be able to infer each word's meaning from the panel that uses it, using picture + sentence-context together.
- For every target word, the panel that introduces it must contain at least one of these anchors: demonstrated_action (picture or caption shows the meaning being performed), near_synonym (caption restates the word in simpler language), category_example (word paired with a category or example), or visible_referent (picture shows the named thing clearly without competing labels).
- No character should lecture about a word ("As you know, this means..."). The anchor must hide inside natural prose.
- Failure example: caption "The line at the comics convention was three hours long" + dialogue "We'll never reach the front" + picture of a crowd. A learner can't tell whether "convention" means "long line", "crowd", "wait", or "event". No anchor.
- Success example: caption "Deep in the dirt, a busy underground city. This is the ants' colony" + picture of a cross-section of tunnels full of ants. Learner can map "colony" to "busy underground city of ants". Anchor type: near_synonym.

Page 1 — Establishing beat:
- The first panel on page 1 must be a wide or establishing shot that orients the reader: show WHERE (the setting), WHO (the main characters), and WHAT (the situation or mood). Include a brief narration caption that grounds the reader before the action begins.
- This is the "once upon a time" moment. Keep it to one panel — don't burn the whole page on setup.

Page 6 — Resolution pacing:
- Do not rush the resolution. Allow at least one panel for the moment of tension or uncertainty BEFORE the resolution lands. The reader needs to feel the stakes before relief.
- End with a reflective or closing beat — a moment of calm, a character's reaction, or a brief thought that signals the story is complete. This eases the transition to the review page.

Text budget:
